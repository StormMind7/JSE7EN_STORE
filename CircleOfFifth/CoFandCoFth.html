What Is the Circle of Fifths?

The Circle of Fifths arranges all 12 musical keys in a clock-like diagram. Moving clockwise, each key is a perfect fifth (7 semitones) higher than the previous. Moving counterclockwise, each key is a perfect fourth (5 semitones) higher — forming the Circle of Fourths. They are the same circle, read in opposite directions.

How Fifths Are Built

  • Start at C (0 sharps/flats)
  • Add a perfect 5th (7 semitones): C→G
  • Each step clockwise adds 1 sharp to the key signature
  • After 12 steps you return to C (12×7 = 84 = 7 octaves)
  • This works because 7 and 12 are coprime

How Fourths Are Built

  • Start at C and move counterclockwise
  • Add a perfect 4th (5 semitones): C→F
  • Each step counterclockwise adds 1 flat
  • F=1♭, B♭=2♭, E♭=3♭ … C♭=7♭
  • Used extensively in jazz & bass-line writing

The Rings Explained

  • Outer ring: Major keys
  • Inner ring: Relative minor keys (same notes, different root)
  • Enharmonic zone: B=C♭, F#=G♭, C#=D♭ (same sound, different name)
  • Adjacent keys share 6 of 7 notes — making modulation smooth

Sharp Order (FCGDAEB)

The sharps always appear in this order:
F♯ C♯ G♯ D♯ A♯ E♯ B♯
Mnemonic: Father Charles Goes Down And Ends Battle

Flat Order (BEADGCF)

The flats always appear in this order:
B♭ E♭ A♭ D♭ G♭ C♭ F♭
Mnemonic: Battle Ends And Down Goes Charles Father (reverse of sharps)

Reading the Circle — A Map

Think of the circle as a map of musical relationships. The closer two keys are on the circle, the more harmonically related they are — they share more notes, and moving between them feels natural. Keys on opposite sides (a tritone apart) share the fewest notes and create the most contrast.

Clockwise = Dominant Motion

  • Each clockwise step is the V chord of the next key
  • C → G: G is the V of C
  • This is the strongest resolution in Western music
  • Chord progressions often "travel clockwise" (V→I)
  • Jazz: ii–V–I is a clockwise journey

Counterclockwise = Subdominant

  • Each counterclockwise step is the IV chord
  • C → F: F is the IV of C
  • Subdominant motion feels "lifting" or "expansive"
  • Gospel/soul music heavily uses IV→I ("plagal cadence")
  • Blues often relies on I–IV–V movement

The Inner Minor Ring

  • Every major key has a relative minor on the inner ring
  • They share the same key signature (same notes)
  • A minor is relative to C major
  • To find it: count down a minor 3rd (3 semitones) from the major root
  • Or: the 6th degree of the major scale

Sharps & Flats at a Glance

  • Right side (3–6 o'clock): sharp keys (G, D, A, E, B, F#)
  • Left side (6–9 o'clock): flat keys (F, B♭, E♭, A♭, D♭, G♭)
  • Top (12 o'clock): C major — zero accidentals
  • Bottom (6 o'clock): F♯/G♭ — 6 sharps or 6 flats
  • More accidentals ≠ harder to play; just more character

Using It for Modulation

  • Diatonic modulation: move 1 step — very smooth
  • Mediant modulation: move 3 steps — colourful leap
  • Tritone substitution: jump 6 steps — jazz technique
  • Pivot chord: find a chord common to both keys to bridge them
  • Parallel major/minor: same root, different mode

Harmonic Analysis with the Circle

When analysing a piece, first identify the tonal centre (key). Then map each chord to its Roman numeral function. Common progressions follow predictable paths on the circle — learning to see these patterns lets you analyse (and compose) quickly.

The ii–V–I (Jazz)

The most important jazz progression. In C major: Dm7 – G7 – Cmaj7. On the circle: move counterclockwise twice (G→C = one fifth). The ii chord prepares the V; the V resolves to I. Every jazz standard uses this.

I–IV–V (Blues/Rock)

The backbone of rock and blues. In C: C – F – G. F is one step counterclockwise; G is one step clockwise from C. These three chords span only 2 adjacent positions on the circle.

I–V–vi–IV (Pop)

The "four-chord song." In C: C – G – Am – F. G (clockwise), Am (relative minor of C), F (counterclockwise). Works in every key and never gets old because of its emotional balance: bright→tense→melancholic→lift.

Analyzing Borrowed Chords

  • A chord from outside the key = borrowed chord
  • Often borrowed from the parallel minor (e.g., ♭VII in major)
  • On the circle: a chord "reaching across" to another key's territory
  • Creates color and surprise without fully modulating

Identifying the Key from Chords

  • Find the dominant 7th chord (V7) — its resolution tells you the key
  • Look for the leading tone (7th scale degree, a half-step below root)
  • The tritone in a V7 chord "points" to the tonic
  • Check the key signature: count sharps/flats → consult the circle

Chord Construction Rules

  • Triad: Root + 3rd + 5th
  • Major: Root + M3 + P5 (e.g. C–E–G)
  • Minor: Root + m3 + P5 (e.g. C–E♭–G)
  • Diminished: Root + m3 + d5 (e.g. C–E♭–G♭)
  • Augmented: Root + M3 + A5 (e.g. C–E–G♯)
  • Dom7: Root + M3 + P5 + m7
  • Maj7: Root + M3 + P5 + M7
  • Min7: Root + m3 + P5 + m7

Voice Leading Tips

  • Move voices by the smallest possible interval
  • Contrary motion between bass and soprano = strength
  • Resolve the leading tone (7th degree) upward to the octave
  • Avoid parallel 5ths and octaves in classical style
  • In jazz: 3rds and 7ths are the "guide tones" — keep them smooth
  • The root can leap; inner voices should step

Secondary Dominants

  • Any diatonic chord can be preceded by its own V7
  • V/V (five of five) = tonicises the dominant
  • In C major: V/ii = A7 → Dm
  • On the circle: a secondary dominant is always one step clockwise of its target
  • Creates a brief "glimpse" into another key
